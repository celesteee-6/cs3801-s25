Surveillance and Civil Liberties

Surveillance technology has grown rapidly in the past decade. It’s used for tracking phones, scanning faces, and monitoring online activity. While it helps prevent crime and manage cities, it also threatens civil liberties like privacy and freedom of speech. This creates an ethical dilemma: how do we balance safety and privacy?

The Utilitarian Perspective

Utilitarianism suggests that the right action is the one that creates the most good for the most people. If surveillance prevents crime and improves safety for many, it may be considered acceptable, even if it invades some privacy. But if it causes fear, discomfort, or unfair targeting, the harm might outweigh the benefits. It’s not always clear which side has the greater impact.

Real-World Examples

China

China’s facial recognition tech tracks people, controls crowds, and affects social credit scores. While the government claims it promotes safety, it also silences dissent and limits freedom. From a utilitarian angle, if millions are scared or restricted to catch a few criminals, the harm may be greater than the good.

London

London is one of the most surveilled cities in the world. While CCTV use is extensive, there are systems for public oversight. This balance between safety and privacy is closer to a fair utilitarian model, trying to help many while respecting individual rights.

Practical Takeaways

For surveillance to be ethical, we need transparency, oversight, and clear rules. Citizens should be informed when and why surveillance is used. Governments should be required to obtain warrants for certain data and ensure surveillance isn’t targeting specific groups unfairly. Independent watchdogs and public input can help keep surveillance systems accountable.

What You Can Do

Ask questions: Am I being watched? Who stores this data? Can I opt out? Support organizations like the ACLU and EFF that fight for digital rights. Be thoughtful about what data you share and advocate for laws that protect privacy in your community.
